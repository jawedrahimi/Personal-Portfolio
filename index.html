I am an Electrical Engineering and Computer Sciences student at UC Berkeley focused on building systems that stay correct and stable under real-world constraints. My work spans low-level systems, embedded hardware, and distributed execution—areas where correctness depends not only on what a system does, but when it does it under noise, latency, and concurrency.

My academic preparation emphasizes implementation-level reasoning: instruction timing, cache coherence, synchronization, communication channel behavior, and device-level effects like thermal variation and voltage noise. These experiences shaped how I approach engineering—treating timing, resource contention, and failure surfaces as fundamental design elements rather than afterthoughts.

My research centers on temporal correctness in distributed cyber-physical systems. I design automated, hardware-in-the-loop testing pipelines that execute programs on embedded devices, capture logical-time traces, detect timing violations, and compare temporal execution behavior across compiler versions. This work aims to turn timing assumptions into measurable, enforceable guarantees.

Beyond research, I work on embedded sensing and automation platforms for sustainable agriculture, designing real-time monitoring systems and control loops that must remain reliable despite environmental drift and hardware imperfections. I also have industry experience developing low-level software modules, strengthening automation systems, and implementing reproducible testing and CI workflows.

Across everything I build, my goal is consistent: to create systems that are deterministic, observable, and resilient—even when the physical world introduces uncertainty.
